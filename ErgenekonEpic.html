Ergenekon Epic

  • Baha Erdoğan
  • History
  • 0

First and foremost, I must clarify that I am not a Turkologist. As a Turkish intellectual, this text has been written out of a need that arose from the difficulties I face in providing resources to my non-Turkish friends.

For a long time, a fierce war raged between the Turks and the Mongols. In these battles, the ever-numerous Turks always emerged victorious. At the helm of the Turks was the ruler known as the “Il Khan,” while leading the Mongols was Sevinç Khan. Sevinç Khan managed to draw the Kyrgyz and other tribes to his side. After a confrontation marred by treachery, the Turks faced defeat. This loss resulted in the slaughter of all Turks, leading to the near extinction of their lineage. However, the youngest son of the Il Khan, named Kıyan, and his cousin Nüküz were taken captive

One day, the two of them managed to escape from their place of captivity. They met in the area where their tribes had once camped. Along with them, the camels, horses, oxen, and sheep that had also escaped the enemy’s clutches gathered at that place. Fearing that if they remained too long, they would easily be found and killed, they resolved to journey with their herds to uncharted lands where no one had set foot before, thus erasing their traces. They followed the paths of wild goats and climbed the mountains. Eventually, they reached a hill beyond the mountain and passed through a narrow passage. Upon looking back, they realized they had crossed a treacherous spot that even a nimble goat would struggle to traverse.

The place they arrived at was vast and untouched, devoid of paths. There, a variety of fruits, plants, animals to hunt, and flowing waters abounded. They thanked God, settled in, and named this land Ergenekon.

In this new home, the lineage of Kıyan and Nüküz flourished. Kıyan's lineage became more numerous. Kıyan’s sons took on the name Kıyat, while Nüküz's sons were known as Nüküzler and Düzlügin. The name Kıyan signified “the flood that descends violently from the mountain.”

Four hundred years passed, their lineage grew increasingly populous, and they could no longer be contained within Ergenekon. Deciding to break free, they proclaimed, “We are many now; we fear no enemy! We will engage with friends and wrestle with foes!”

The path out was obscured. A wolf appeared, paused briefly, then left; it returned, lingered a while, and left again. A wise elder recognized that the Grey Wolf had come from the Gökbörü. They followed the trail of the Grey Wolf. They traveled a long road, until the Grey Wolf halted at a particular spot. There, the blacksmith noticed that the ground was covered by a thin layer of iron. They ignited a great fire, melting the iron, and the pioneers emerged from the molten earth.

Finding the outside world secure, all the people rejoiced and emerged from Ergenekon. They never forgot this day; ancestors told the tales to their descendants, and sons shared them with their grandchildren. This day became a day of celebration for generations.
