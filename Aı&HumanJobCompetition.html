Will Artificial Intelligence Replace My Job?

  • Baha Erdoğan
  • AI
  • 0

I observed that during discussions with friends, the conversation often drifts to one question: Will AI replace jobs? While AI has enormous capabilities and wide application, its impact on employment remains one of the most frequent concerns.

As someone who started working on AI engineering long before ChatGPT existed, I try to keep my original perspective—avoiding both hype and panic. My approach focuses on two questions: feasibility and cost.

You're Likely Safe If

  • Your work requires movement and dexterity more than raw physical strength.
  • Your role involves significant representation or human interaction (face-to-face communication, client-facing work).
  • Your total cost to the employer is low.
  • The job inherently requires continuous supervision.

You're at High Risk If

  • Most of your work is sedentary and cognitive, and you are new or not yet at the top of your field.
  • Your total cost to the employer (salary, benefits, safety, meals, transport, visa, training, etc.) is high.

The Cost Factor

When AI’s hardware and adaptation costs decrease while its output increases, the incentive to automate grows. If replacing you with AI is cheaper than keeping you employed, change accelerates.

Figures already reflect this: in finance, over 70% of certain analyst tasks can be performed by AI. On the factory floor, when operating costs of robots (e.g., Baxter) undercut wages and amortize favorably, adoption speeds up.

Who's Safer in the Short Term?

Workers in low-wage economies may not face immediate displacement. However, as advanced robotics and AI reduce costs further in developed countries, production tends to reshore—widening the gap.

To see the full picture, consider why production moved to developing countries in the first place (cost advantage), and how that advantage shifts when automation and energy costs favor developed economies.

If this topic interests you, also read: Value Per Kilogram for Prosperity.

All Things Considered

If your work creates tangible value that doesn’t fit the mass‑production paradigm—craftsmanship, coding with complex context, surgery, dentistry, hairstyling—full replacement is less likely. But if your work can be taught step‑by‑step to someone with limited training (and that makes up most of your job), you should plan transitions.

A Personal Story

On a visit to a European factory automation company, I was told they could produce almost entirely without human labor. Yet, through a large window into the production floor, I saw people doing most of the work. That reinforced my view: if a task cannot fit into a mass‑production model, AI may not replace it soon.
